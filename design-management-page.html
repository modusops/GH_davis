    Shutterstock Collections

    Shutterstock strives to be the leading workflow platform for digital creatives. I was responsible for driving product initiatives for the MVP cloud collaborative experience, working with stakeholders and interviewing customers to define the MVP. Our goals for the project included:


  • 1. Obtain an understanding of how Shutterstock fits into the the creative workflow
  • 2. Map user journeys and create a backlog of user stories from user research
  • 3. Create prototypes and validate design solutions meeting MVP requirements

Objective: Gather information to define the MVP and create a backlog of user stories.

Shutterstock wanted to learn how its Collections product fit into their customer's creative workflow, in order to create a collaborative product that would fit the customer needs. I helped lead the effort to obtain, synthesize, and design an MVP product for this new initiative.



To kickoff the UX process, I met with key stakeholders to gain an understanding of what the project goals were and what problems we were hoping to solve.



Hypothesis: Shutterstock plays a small but crucial part of the creative workflow. However, there are too many steps in the design and ideation process. In addition, it is difficult to collaborate on images within the Collections product, especially to gain feedback and approval.

Define

Working with stakeholders, we identified one major scenario for which we wished to investigate the customer experience.



After a discussion of the project brief, the product manager and I held a kickoff with the larger team. We reviewed some preliminary ideas that were wireframed that helped spark additional conversations about scope and technical feasibility.



Result: We agreed that the design of the page should utilize components from the current Shutterstock design library and that any features requiring new components should be reserved for post MVP releases.

Research

As a part of the research process, we analyzed other cloud collaboration products. In addition, we interviewed a current Shutterstock customer who was an active Collections user. The customer also fit the description of a user who might be interested in our creative cloud collaboration product.



We invited our customer to the research lab and held a working session to learn more about the workflow and where Shutterstock would fit in. We also probed for painpoints in the customer journey.


From the working session, we generated a simple story map and 3 product roadmaps filled with features that mapped back to user stories.



Result: With roadmaps and features in the backlog, we were ready to begin the design process and create solutions for each user story.

Design

From user interviews, we were able to generate a conceptual model of how users came in contact with Shutterstock and how Collections fit with their creative workflow.



With job stories we had generated from the research, we began exploring different solutions and mapped them back to how customers feel at different parts of the journey.


We also began exploring the interaction designs and conducted internal user tests to validate our proposals.


A list view was designed after learning this mode was preferred amongst more advanced users.


Interaction design concepts for hover states, dropdown menu items, bulk selection mode, and renaming collections.


We learned that Shutterstock Collections users enjoyed using the space as a moodboard, and re-arranging their images was a highly desired feature.


The final MVP design for Shutterstock's new design management page.


Specification for components




Result: Shutterstock's design management page launched in late 2016.
